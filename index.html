The Rider

A young Nepali who found motorcycles at age 12, bought his first KTM Duke 250 at 16, and taught himself to ride in two days navigating Kathmandu's chaotic traffic. Today, he trains at one of Asia's finest circuits, carrying Nepal's flag on the world racing stage.

Background

Life Story

Age 12 — The Spark (Early 2010s)

A Boy Finds His Dream

Growing up in Kathmandu, Nepal, life was full of hills, valleys, and the constant hum of motorbikes weaving through the city's legendary traffic. For most kids his age, bikes were just transport. For him, they were something else entirely.

At age 12, something clicked. He saw a motorcycle — the way it leaned into a corner, the raw sound, the freedom it represented — and in that moment, a direction was set. Not just a hobby. A calling. From that point, every conversation drifted back to bikes, every saved pocket money went toward bike magazines, and every free hour was spent watching racing videos and dreaming of the track.

The mountains of Nepal had given him fearlessness. Now, they would give him his first wheels.

Age 16 — The First Machine

KTM Duke 250: Two Days to Tame Kathmandu

The day he turned 16, the plan was already in motion. With savings carefully accumulated and a determination that outpaced his years, he bought his first motorcycle: a KTM Duke 250 — an Austrian-engineered street fighter known for its raw, responsive ride and unforgiving feedback. Not a learner bike. Not a compromise.

Most riders take weeks — sometimes months — to find their confidence. He learned in two days, thrown headfirst into the organised chaos of Kathmandu's streets. Traffic in the Nepali capital is a living, breathing maze: tuk-tuks darting across lanes, pedestrians stepping off pavements, motorcycles packed in three-deep at every junction. For many, it's terrifying. For him, it was his first circuit.

Those two days on Kathmandu's roads gave him something no formal training could: instinct under pressure. The ability to read traffic, anticipate gaps, and make split-second decisions — skills that would later translate directly to race conditions.

16–20 — Finding the Racing World

Into the Biker Circles of Nepal

As he grew more confident on the Duke, word spread naturally among Nepal's tight-knit biking community. He started connecting with local biker circles — weekend rides through mountain passes, impromptu track days, and spirited group runs across Nepal's winding roads.

These circles weren't just social — they were an informal academy. Experienced riders shared technique, cornering philosophy, and the kind of unwritten knowledge that lives only in the hands of those who've spent thousands of hours on two wheels. He absorbed it all.

Gradually, the casual rides gave way to something more structured. Local track sessions and informal races revealed what the street had already suggested: he was faster than most. His natural spatial awareness and fearless throttle hand stood out, and it wasn't long before the idea of competitive racing went from background thought to active pursuit.

He began training more seriously — riding Nepal's mountain roads as his personal test track, developing racecraft instincts in the absence of a formal circuit. Nepal itself became his training ground, its altitude, terrain, and traffic building a foundation unlike anything a conventional racing school could replicate.

2023 — The Breakthrough

2NNRC & The Asia Talent Cup

2023 was the year it all came together. Competing in the Nepal National Racing Championship (2NNRC), he lined up against the country's most competitive circuit riders and delivered a campaign that announced him as a genuine national talent — not just a prospect, but a racer.

More significantly, the same year brought a selection call that few Nepali riders had ever received: pre-selection for the Idemitsu Asia Talent Cup, Dorna Sports' prestigious feeder series connecting the best young Asian riders with the MotoGP pipeline. The pre-selection process is rigorous — an audition watched by some of the sport's most experienced eyes. The fact that he made it through the initial screening placed him among an elite group of riders from across an entire continent.

For a self-taught racer from Kathmandu who learned on public roads, reaching this level was not just personal achievement — it was a statement about what Nepali motorsport was becoming.

Now — Chennai, India

Madras International Circuit: Levelling Up

Today, he trains at the Madras International Circuit (MIC) in Chennai, India — one of Asia's most respected motorsports facilities, built to FIM standards and home to some of the continent's sharpest racing talent.

Currently progressing through Level 1 and Level 2 training, the focus is on translating raw speed into refined racecraft: braking markers, apex geometry, late-corner drives, body positioning, and tyre management. The facility and coaching infrastructure represent the most structured environment he has ever trained in — and his progress reflects it.

The journey from a 12-year-old watching bikes in Kathmandu to a serious international prospect training at MIC is one of the most compelling rising stories in South Asian motorsport. The next chapter is still being written — at full throttle.
